📈 Credit & Finance

Build business credit, master finances, and unlock funding

Where are you on your financial journey?

Answer honestly — your roadmap will be customized to your starting point.

The 10-Step Credit & Finance Roadmap

1

Pull All 3 Credit Reports

Get free reports at AnnualCreditReport.com. Review Equifax, Experian, and TransUnion for errors, negative items, and accounts you don't recognize.

2

Dispute Inaccurate Items

Write formal dispute letters to each bureau. Under the FCRA, bureaus must investigate and respond within 30 days. Keep copies of everything.

3

Set Up a Debt Payoff Plan

Use the Snowball Method (smallest balance first) or Avalanche Method (highest interest first). Both work — choose the one you'll actually stick to.

4

Open a Secured Credit Card

If rebuilding, a secured card (deposit-backed) lets you build positive payment history. Use it for small purchases and pay in full monthly.

5

Build a 3-Month Emergency Fund

Start with $1,000, then build to 3 months of expenses. Keep it in a separate high-yield savings account. This protects credit during setbacks.

6

Get Your EIN & Open a Business Bank Account

Register your EIN free at IRS.gov. Open a dedicated business checking account immediately — this is the foundation of your business financial identity.

7

Register with Business Credit Bureaus

Set up profiles with Dun & Bradstreet (DUNS number), Experian Business, and Equifax Business. These are separate from your personal credit.

8

Open Net-30 Vendor Accounts

Apply for vendor accounts that report to business credit bureaus (Uline, Quill, Grainger). Pay on or before the due date to build your Paydex score.

9

Apply for a Business Credit Card

Once you have 3–6 months of business banking history and vendor accounts, apply for a business credit card. Use it for business expenses only.

10

Pursue Funding & Grants

With solid credit and financials, apply for SBA microloans, CDFI loans, MBDA grants, and local small business programs. Keep your documents ready.

Financial Wisdom Library

💳 The 3-Bank Method

Keep three separate accounts: (1) Operations — for bills and recurring expenses. (2) Profit — 10% of every payment received goes here, never touched. (3) Growth — for reinvestment and opportunities. This simple separation prevents cash flow chaos and builds wealth automatically.

🤝 ROSCAs — Rotating Savings & Credit Associations

A centuries-old community banking method used across Africa, the Caribbean, and Latin America. A group of trusted people each contribute a fixed amount monthly; one member receives the full pot each month. Known as "sou-sou" in the Caribbean and "partner" in Jamaica, ROSCAs build financial discipline and provide lump-sum capital without interest. Best for trusted, small groups with clear agreements in writing.

🏦 Black Banking History & Why It Matters

Black-owned banks were established after Reconstruction to serve communities locked out of mainstream banking. The Freedman's Bank (1865), though it failed due to mismanagement, inspired generations of Black banking pioneers. Today, Black-owned CDFIs and banks like OneUnited Bank, Carver Federal, and Liberty Bank offer business loans, mortgages, and credit-building products specifically designed for Black entrepreneurs. Banking Black keeps capital circulating in Black communities.

📊 The 50/30/20 Budget Rule

Allocate 50% of after-tax income to needs (rent, utilities, food), 30% to wants (entertainment, dining out), and 20% to savings and debt repayment. For business owners, shift this to 40/20/40 — directing 40% toward growth and savings to build wealth faster.

✅ Recommendation for Most H.E.L.P. Center Clients

Start with an LLC. It protects your personal assets, is simple to maintain, and gives you flexibility to elect S-Corp taxation later when your profits justify it. File your Articles of Organization with your state Secretary of State — usually $50–$150.

Formation Checklist

Choose a business name — check availability on your state's SOS website and USPTO.gov for trademarks
File Articles of Organization — with your state Secretary of State
Get your EIN — free at IRS.gov, takes 5 minutes online
Draft an Operating Agreement — even for single-member LLCs
Open a business bank account — requires EIN + Articles of Organization
Register for state/local business licenses — check your city and county requirements
Get a registered agent — required in most states (you can be your own)

Brand & Digital Presence

🎨 Brand Identity Essentials

Your brand is more than a logo — it's how people feel when they interact with you. Define: (1) Your core transformation (what problem do you solve?), (2) Your ideal client, (3) Your brand voice (professional? warm? bold?), (4) 2–3 brand colors + 1–2 fonts. Keep it consistent everywhere.

🌐 Website Minimum Viable Pages

A professional website needs: Home (hero + value proposition), About (your story + credentials), Services (clear offerings + pricing or "contact for pricing"), Testimonials, and Contact. Add a booking link (Calendly, Acuity) from day one.

📱 Social Media Strategy

You don't need to be everywhere. Pick 1–2 platforms where your ideal client spends time. For service businesses, LinkedIn + Instagram work well. Post consistently (3x/week) before quantity matters. Lead with education and transformation, not just promotions.

Digital Presence Checklist

Claim your domain name — .com preferred, yourname.com or yourbusiness.com
Set up professional email — yourname@yourbusiness.com (Google Workspace or similar)
Launch a website — even a simple one-pager is better than none
Set up Google Business Profile — free and critical for local visibility
Choose and set up 1–2 social platforms — complete all profile fields
Get a professional headshot — natural light works fine
Create a simple services menu / rate sheet — know your pricing before you quote

Weekly Leadership Challenges

One action per week. Small moves build massive confidence.

Week 1: Speak First

In your next meeting or group setting, speak up first or second — before you talk yourself out of it. Your voice matters.

Journal prompt: What did it feel like? What reaction did you get?

Week 2: Raise Your Rate

Identify one service you're undercharging for and increase the price by at least 20%. Send an updated proposal or announcement this week.

Journal prompt: What fear came up? Did anyone actually leave?

Week 3: Say No Once

Decline one request, opportunity, or commitment that doesn't align with your goals — without over-explaining or apologizing.

Journal prompt: How did it feel? What opened up when you said no?

Week 4: Ask for the Room

Request a speaking opportunity, panel seat, podcast interview, or collaboration with someone whose platform is bigger than yours.

Journal prompt: Did they say yes? What happened when you simply asked?

Mindset Library

🧠 Imposter Syndrome Is a Liar

Imposter syndrome — the feeling that you'll be "found out" as incompetent — affects 70% of high achievers. Research by Pauline Clance shows it's most common among high-performing women and people of color. The antidote: collect evidence of competence, not feelings about it. Keep a "wins file" — a list of real accomplishments you can review when doubt surfaces.

💎 The Authority Principle

People follow those who act like leaders before they're recognized as one. Leadership isn't a title — it's a posture, a voice, a consistency of action. You build authority by: showing up prepared, making decisions, taking responsibility, and doing what you said you'd do. Every time. Authority is earned in small moments, not big announcements.

🔥 Confidence Is a Skill, Not a Feeling

Waiting to feel confident before acting is backwards. Confidence comes from doing the thing — especially when you're afraid. Take the action first. Analyze the result. Adjust. Repeat. Over time, your evidence base grows and the fear shrinks. Act your way into the feeling, not the other way around.

🌊 The Power of Regulation

Leaders who can self-regulate — managing their emotional responses under pressure — are consistently rated higher by their teams. Techniques: box breathing (4-4-4-4 count) before high-stakes conversations, naming the emotion ("I notice I'm feeling anxious"), and creating a pause between stimulus and response. Regulation is a leadership superpower.
